Planning Your Funeral: A Complete Guide to Your Options

Plan Your Funeral With Confidence

A clear, simple guide to understanding funeral, disposition, and memorialization options. Designed for individuals with no prior funeral-service knowledge.

Explore your options

Ceremony Options

Home Funeral

A home funeral allows family and friends to care for the body in a private home. It can include washing, dressing, viewing, spiritual rituals, storytelling, or quiet time. Embalming is not required.

Funeral Service

A structured ceremony where the body is present. It may include a visitation, embalming (optional), readings, music, and cultural or religious traditions. After the funeral, the chosen form of disposition follows.

Memorial Service

A ceremony held without the body present. This option allows flexibility and can occur weeks or months after death at a venue of the family's choosing.

Graveside Service

A simple ceremony held outdoors at the burial site. It may involve prayers, readings, military honors, or symbolic gestures such as placing flowers or soil.

Burial at Sea

A meaningful option for those connected to the ocean. Cremated remains or whole bodies may be placed at sea in accordance with federal regulations. Families may accompany the vessel depending on provider policies.

Disposition Options (What Happens to the Body)

Below are legal and common forms of final disposition. Each method is compatible with various services and personalization choices.

Traditional Burial

Traditional burial typically includes a casket, cemetery plot, and a vault or grave liner. Embalming is optional but often chosen for viewing.

Green or Natural Burial

An environmentally friendly option using biodegradable materials and no vault. Embalming is not permitted unless non-toxic solutions are used.

Cremation Overview

Cremation reduces remains using heat. Families may choose services before or after the process.

Cremation Containers

A cremation container is required and may be cardboard, wood, or biodegradable material.

Urn Options

Urns come in many types including wood, metal, ceramic, biodegradable, keepsake urns, scattering tubes, and custom art urns.

Burial of Cremated Remains

Cremated remains can be buried in a cemetery plot, interred in a niche, or placed in a natural burial ground.

Alkaline Hydrolysis (Aquamation)

A water-based form of disposition using warm water and alkali to accelerate natural decomposition.

Container Requirements

A simple biodegradable shroud or container may be used. No casket is required.

Urns and Memorial Options

Families receive processed remains similar to cremation and may choose burial, scattering, or memorial products.

Natural Organic Reduction (Human Composting)

The body is placed in a vessel with organic materials and transformed into soil over several weeks.

Container Requirements

A biodegradable shroud is used. Embalming is not permitted. No casket or vault is used.

Soil Options

Families may take home all soil, receive a portion, or donate some or all for land restoration.

Memorialization Options

Merchandise-Based Memorialization

There are many creative ways to honor a life after disposition. Options include mushroom or infinity burial suits, tree pods, art glass, eternal reef structures, vinyl records made with cremated remains, keepsake jewelry, paintings infused with ashes, or walking sticks with a scattering compartment.

Service-Based Memorialization

Experience-centered options include space flight memorials, reef placement ceremonies, soil donation for forest restoration, witness cremation events, or family participation in natural or green burial activities. Families may accompany the placement of reef structures depending on provider availability.

Understanding All Available Choices

Many people request a simple cremation without realizing it involves several decisions. These may include whether to have a ceremony before or after cremation, whether to witness the cremation process, what type of container or urn they prefer, and how they want the remains memorialized or placed. This guide helps individuals understand all available options before choosing.

Personalizing Your Arrangements

Every ceremony and disposition can be customized. Options include music, storytelling, cultural rituals, custom clothing, memory tables, symbolic gestures, special locations for services, biodegradable urn choices, or family participation in washing, dressing, or lowering the body.

Compatibility and Restrictions

Some selections are not compatible. Embalming cannot be used for natural organic reduction and is usually not permitted for green burial unless biodegradable fluids are used. Some whole-body sea burials prohibit embalming. Cemetery rules may require vaults for traditional burials. Cultural and religious considerations may restrict certain methods such as cremation.

Final Questions to Consider

  • Do you want your body present at any ceremonies?
  • Would you prefer a traditional, natural, or environmentally focused option?
  • Do you want a funeral before disposition or a memorial after?
  • How do you want your remains or soil handled long-term?
  • What memorialization options reflect your personality and values?
  • Do any selections conflict with each other?
  • Have you communicated your wishes clearly in writing?

This resource is designed to help individuals make thoughtful, informed, and meaningful decisions about their final arrangements. For questions or assistance, consult a licensed funeral professional in your state.
